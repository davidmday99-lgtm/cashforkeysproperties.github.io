Selling a House With Code Violations

Published: July 20, 2026 By: Cash For Keys Properties

Open code violations can arise from property maintenance, unsafe conditions, or work completed without approval. They do not always prevent a sale, but they can affect financing, title work, costs, and the closing timeline.

Request the Official Records

Contact the local building or code enforcement department for the written notice, inspection history, correction requirements, deadlines, and any fines or liens. Work from official records instead of relying on assumptions.

Clarify What Transfers at Closing

Some violations must be corrected before transfer, while others may be assumed by a buyer under specific procedures. A local title company, attorney, or real estate professional can explain the rules that apply to the property.

Price the Required Work

Obtain estimates from properly qualified contractors and ask whether plans, permits, reinspection fees, or code upgrades are included. The least expensive quote may not cover everything needed to close the case.

Keep Buyers Fully Informed

Provide notices, estimates, permits, and correspondence you have received. Clear documentation helps buyers evaluate the property and reduces the chance of surprises during due diligence.

Consider an As-Is Buyer

A cash buyer familiar with distressed properties may be able to account for code corrections in the offer. Compare the net proceeds, responsibility for open items, contingencies, and closing timing in writing.
